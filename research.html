Our Research

Five Pillars

We explore how and why microbial communities change and adapt through time. Our research team is founded on five main pillars of research that survey microbial communities in anything from Antarctic ice to a human tooth.

1. Ancient Human Microbiota

How did large cultural and dietary revolutions impact our microbiota? Where and how did both infectious and non-infectious diseases originate? Did microbes in the human body influence human evolution? Did the development of complex societies irreversibly alter the future of human microbiota? Our research explores ancient human microbiota evolution, adaptation, over time to better understand the origin microbes in the human body.

2. Ancient Animal Microbiota

How do microbes aid in animal adaptation to past environments? Did domestication impact animal microbiota? Can ancient DNA from animals tell us about past ecology? Do humans and microbes share microbiota, and if so, did animal domestication influence this? We examine ancient microbiota in animals to learn more about past animal health and diet.

3. Ancient Environmental Microbiota

Did microbes in the soil shift with climate change? How did human activities impact environmental microbes? Did volcanic activity in Antarctica shift the global climate? Are ancient environmental microbes markers of past environmental and climatic events? We examine ancient environmental DNA (eDNA) in soils, sediment, calcite, and ice to learn more about how biological change in the past.

4. Modern Human Oral Microbiome In Health And Disease

Do our different lifestyles impact the microbes that live in our mouths? How does Industrialization or a ‘Western’ diet shift the microbes in my mouth? How do communities of microbes work together to cause cavities and periodontal disease? How are micorbes in communities today linked to the microbes in our ancestors? We examine the oral microbiota in living people to better understand the past and to help improve dental health in the future.

5. Model Systems To Understand Oral Microbiota

How do we grow diverse communities of microbes? Can we combine visualizations of microbial biofilms with DNA analyses to better understand microbial communities? How do microbial communities collectively respond to changes in nutrients and their local environment? We use mixtures of mouse and in vitro models to explore how and why microbial communities adapt and change over time.

Current Projects

World Wide Ancient Oral Microbiome Diversity

We are working to reconstruct the evolutionary history of the human oral microbiome on five continents over the past 50,000 years.

Oral Microbiome Transplant Therapy

Our international team, in partnership with the University of Adelaide, is developing oral microbiota transplant (OMT) therapy for possible treatment of severe cavities or periodontal disease.
